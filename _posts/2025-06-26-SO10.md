## Rotation Group SO(n)

For any [anti-symmetric matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew-symmetric_matrix) \\( \omega \\) with \\( \omega^\intercal=-\omega \\) the [matrix exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_exponential) \\( \exp(\omega) \\) is a rotation matrix, that leaves the scalar product between two vectors invariant since:

$$
  \exp(\omega)^\intercal \exp(\omega) = \exp(\omega^\intercal+\omega) = \bm{1}
$$

> The n-dimensional rotation group consisting of all \\( \exp(\omega) \\) is called SO(n) and is generated by the \\( \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \\) independent components of a n-dimensional anti-symmetric matrix \\( \omega \\). SO(n) is a subgroup of the orthogonal group O(n) that also includes reflections.

## Clifford Algebra

The [Clifford algebra](https://niklasbuschmann.github.io/multiplying-vectors/) consists of products of \\( \bm{\gamma_i} \\) that obey the following anti-commutation relation:

$$
  \frac{1}{2}\{\bm{\gamma_i},\bm{\gamma_i}\} = \bm{1}\delta_{ij}
$$

This means that for any \\( i \neq j \\):

$$
  \bm{\gamma_i}\bm{\gamma_i} = \bm{1} \qquad \bm{\gamma_i}\bm{\gamma_j} = -\bm{\gamma_j}\bm{\gamma_i}
$$

From this anti-commutation relation one can show that the \\( \bm{\gamma_i} \\) rotate like vectors when sandwiched between two exponentials of \\( \omega_{ij}\bm{\gamma_i}\bm{\gamma_j} \\):

$$
  \exp(-\omega_{ij}\bm{\gamma_i}\bm{\gamma_j}/2)\bm{\gamma_\alpha}\exp(\omega_{ij}\bm{\gamma_i}\bm{\gamma_j}/2)=[\exp(\omega)]_{\alpha i}\bm{\gamma_i}
$$

> **Note**: [Einstein summation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation) is used where any index occurs twice.

## Unitary Group U(n)

The argument about the rotation group can be generalized to the unitary group U(n) where instead of \\( O^\intercal O = \bm{1} \\) one wants instead \\( \overline{U}^\intercal U = \bm{1} \\). Assuming that instead of anti-symmetric \\( \omega \\) is now [anti-hermitian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew-Hermitian_matrix) with \\( \overline{\omega}^\intercal=-\omega \\) then again \\( \overline{\exp(\omega)}^\intercal \exp(\omega) = \exp(\overline{\omega}^\intercal+\omega) = \bm{1} \\).

> The n-dimensional unitary group is called U(n) and generated by \\( \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \\) real and \\( \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \\) imaginary degrees of freedom of an anti-hermitian matrix. The subgroup SU(n) includes only unitarian matrices with determinant 1.

Any complex number \\( a + bi \\) can be represented as \\( 2 \times 2 \\) matrix:

$$
  a+bi = a\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix} + b\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ -1 & 0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}a & b\\ -b & a\end{pmatrix}
$$

## Matrix representation of the Clifford algebra

In \\( n \\) dimensions the complete Clifford algebra is of dimension \\( 2^n \\), so its matrix representation must consist of at least \\( 2^{n/2} \times 2^{n/2} \\) matrices. 

One such representation can be found using the [Weyl-Brauer matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl–Brauer_matrices), here for \\( n = 10 \\) dimensions, where the \\( \bm{\gamma_i} \\) and their products are complex \\( 32 \times 32 \\) matrices:

$$
  \bm{\gamma_1}=\bm{\sigma_x} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \qquad \bm{\gamma_2}=\bm{\sigma_y} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \\
  \bm{\gamma_3}=\bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_x} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \qquad \bm{\gamma_4}=\bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_y} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \\
  \bm{\gamma_5}=\bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_x} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \qquad \bm{\gamma_6}=\bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_y} \otimes \bm{1} \otimes \bm{1} \\
  \bm{\gamma_7}=\bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes  \bm{\sigma_x} \otimes \bm{1} \qquad \bm{\gamma_8}=\bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes  \bm{\sigma_y} \otimes \bm{1} \\
  \bm{\gamma_9}=\bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes  \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_x} \qquad \bm{\gamma_{10}}=\bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes  \bm{\sigma_z} \otimes \bm{\sigma_y}
$$

Where the \\( \bm{\sigma_i} \\) are the [Pauli matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_matrices) and \\( \otimes \\) denotes the [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_product):

$$
  \bm{\sigma_x} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} \quad \bm{\sigma_y} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -i\\ i & 0\end{pmatrix} \quad \bm{\sigma_z} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}
$$
